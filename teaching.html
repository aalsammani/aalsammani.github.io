Teaching Philosophy

Mathematics gives us the language to describe complex systems with precision and to reason about them with confidence. In every course I teach, I develop habits of thought that are rigorous, portable, and rooted in first principles. Students learn not only how a method works but why it works, where it fails, and how it connects to questions beyond the classroom. In courses involving machine learning and statistical modeling, I anchor discussion in optimization theory, linear algebra, probability, and statistical inference, asking students to engage with algorithms at the level of their mathematical structure.

Pedagogy

How I Teach

Three principles organize my classroom practice, whether the course is proof-based analysis or applied machine learning.

Theory to Application

Every course follows a deliberate mathematical arc: definitions, axioms, and central theorems first; worked examples and applications as consequences. In proof-based courses, I walk students through the reasoning and dead ends that produce a finished argument. In applied courses, every computational technique is preceded by the result that justifies it, so students see computation as an outgrowth of theory rather than a replacement.

Purposeful Computation

In data science, machine learning, and scientific computation courses, laboratory exercises in Python, R, or MATLAB are tightly coupled to each unit's mathematical content. A representative assignment asks students to derive the gradient update for a regularized regression model, implement it, apply it to clinical data, and interpret results in light of both statistical theory and the subject matter.

Research-Informed Instruction

I draw on my own work in infectious disease modeling, computational neuroscience, and health analytics for examples that show the reach of mathematical methods into biomedical science. When teaching optimization, I illustrate gradient-based techniques through parameter estimation in epidemic models. When teaching probability, I ground conditional reasoning in diagnostic screening problems.

Curriculum Development

Mathematics-Enhanced AI Courses

Proposed graduate and advanced undergraduate courses for Howard University's research cluster in AI for radiology and endocrinology, where strong mathematical preparation is indispensable for responsible AI work.

Course I

Optimization & Numerical Methods for Machine Learning

Gradient descent variants, convex and nonconvex landscapes, and convergence guarantees for training statistical and deep learning models.

Course II

Statistical Learning Theory & Model Validation

Generalization bounds, bias-variance tradeoffs, and model selection in high-dimensional, low-sample biomedical settings.

Course III

Inverse Problems & Image Reconstruction

Ill-posedness, regularization, and iterative solvers applied to medical imaging modalities including CT, MRI, and PET.

Course IV

Uncertainty Quantification in Predictive Systems

Bayesian inference, sensitivity analysis, and uncertainty propagation supporting the reliability of AI-assisted clinical predictions.

Course V

Ethics & Responsibility in Quantitative Modeling

How loss functions, training data composition, and fairness constraints produce measurable consequences for equity in healthcare delivery.

Mentorship

Mentorship & Student Development

I treat mentorship as a natural extension of classroom teaching, guiding students through the full arc of mathematical research.

Undergraduate Research

At Jacksonville University, I supervised several undergraduate research projects in which students formulated applied problems, built predictive models, and presented findings formally. Students learned to:

  • Read literature critically and isolate tractable questions
  • Write precise mathematical formulations
  • Implement and validate computational solutions
  • Communicate results to varied audiences

Graduate Advising

I bring the same commitment to graduate mentoring through qualifying exam preparation, thesis advising, and career-planning conversations built on regular one-on-one meetings and detailed written feedback on proofs, derivations, and drafts.

Commitment to Inclusive Excellence

Supporting students from historically underrepresented groups in the mathematical sciences is a responsibility I hold with particular conviction. I keep accessible office hours, build assignments that develop skill in a graduated sequence, and foster a classroom culture where asking hard questions is expected and respected.

My assessments probe conceptual understanding rather than procedural memory. In AI and data science courses, I engage students with questions of bias, fairness, and the social weight of modeling decisions, because learning to build models responsibly is part of doing applied mathematics well.
