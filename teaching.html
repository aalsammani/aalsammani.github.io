Teaching Philosophy

Mathematics gives us the language to describe complex systems with precision and to reason about them with confidence. In every course I teach, I work to develop habits of thought that are rigorous, portable, and grounded in first principles. Students learn not only how a method works, but why it works, where its assumptions break down, and how it connects to questions beyond the classroom. In applied courses on machine learning, statistical modeling, and scientific computation, I anchor instruction in the underlying theory of optimization, linear algebra, probability, and statistical inference, asking students to engage with algorithms at the level of their mathematical structure rather than treating them as black boxes.

How I Teach

Three principles organize my classroom practice, whether the course is proof-based analysis or applied machine learning.

Theory to Application

Every course follows a deliberate mathematical arc: definitions and central theorems first, worked examples and applications as consequences. In proof-based courses, I walk students through the reasoning that produces a finished argument. In applied courses, every computational technique is preceded by the result that justifies it, so students see computation as an outgrowth of theory rather than a substitute for it.

Purposeful Computation

In data science, machine learning, and scientific computation courses, laboratory exercises in Python, R, or MATLAB are tightly coupled to each unit's mathematical content. A representative assignment asks students to derive the gradient update for a regularized model, implement it, apply it to real data, and interpret the results in light of both statistical theory and the subject matter at hand.

Research-Informed Instruction

I draw on my research in infectious disease modeling, computational neuroscience, and health analytics for examples that demonstrate the reach of mathematical methods in biomedical science. When teaching optimization, I illustrate gradient-based techniques through parameter estimation in epidemic models. When teaching probability, I ground conditional reasoning in diagnostic screening problems familiar to students in health-related fields.

Undergraduate Research & Mentorship

Undergraduate Research Projects

At Jacksonville University, I supervised eight independent undergraduate research projects (MATH 487RI and MATH 490), spanning statistical learning, data analytics, and predictive modeling. Students formulated applied problems, built and validated models, and presented their findings in formal settings. Through this process, students developed the ability to:

  • Read technical literature critically and identify tractable research questions
  • Write precise mathematical and statistical problem formulations
  • Implement, test, and validate computational solutions
  • Communicate methods and results to varied audiences

Commitment to Inclusive Excellence

Supporting students from historically underrepresented groups in the mathematical sciences is a responsibility I hold with conviction. I maintain accessible office hours, design assignments that build skill in a graduated sequence, and foster a classroom environment where rigorous questions are expected and welcomed.

My assessments target conceptual understanding rather than procedural recall. In data science and machine learning courses, I engage students with questions of fairness, bias, and the social weight of modeling decisions, because learning to build models responsibly is part of doing applied mathematics well.
